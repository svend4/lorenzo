## 5. The Economics of Profession-Wide Replication

A defining feature of Professional Colleague Agents is that
**one agent serves all practitioners of a profession**. This
has profound economic implications.

### 5.1. Cost Structure

**Layer A (knowledge base) is high-cost, one-time.** Building
a high-quality knowledge base for a profession requires
substantial expert time. Estimate: 1,000-10,000 expert-hours
to bootstrap, ongoing 100-500 hours/year to maintain.

**Layer B (generation engine) is low marginal cost.** LLM
inference costs continue to drop. With current trends, a
sophisticated Professional Colleague Agent costs perhaps
$5-50 per practitioner per month in LLM API fees, depending
on usage intensity.

**Layer C (interface) is moderate-cost, one-time.** Building
a professional-grade interface costs perhaps 2,000-5,000
developer hours. Ongoing maintenance perhaps 500 hours/year.

So total fixed costs are high (thousands of expert hours)
but per-user costs are low (tens of dollars per month).

### 5.2. Revenue Models

Several viable revenue structures:

**Model 1 — Practitioner Subscription.** Each practitioner
pays directly. Common for individual professionals (lawyers,
accountants, consultants). Requires demonstrating clear ROI
to individual practitioner.

**Model 2 — Institutional License.** Schools, hospitals,
firms pay for entire workforce. Reduces per-practitioner
cost; institution captures aggregate ROI.

**Model 3 — Government Procurement.** State or municipal
government purchases for entire profession. Fits well with
public professions (teachers, social workers, public
defenders).

**Model 4 — Foundation-Funded Public Good.** Mission-driven
foundation funds development and operation; service provided
free or at minimal cost. Particularly fitting for professions
serving vulnerable populations.

**Model 5 — Hybrid.** Free tier for basic functions, paid
tiers for advanced features. «Обучай» appears to use this
model.

**Model 6 — Cooperative Ownership.** Practitioners own the
service collectively, paying nominal subscription that funds
operations. Most aligned with profession's interests, requires
institutional capacity.

For OKWF specifically, Model 4 (foundation-funded) and Model
6 (cooperative) are most aligned with mission. Model 3 is
appropriate for partnerships with public-sector employers.

### 5.3. Network Effects and Knowledge Compounding

A unique property: knowledge compounds. As more practitioners
use the agent, opportunities emerge to learn from collective
patterns:

- Common errors → improved Layer A guidance
- Effective approaches → patterns added to knowledge base
- Edge cases → expanded coverage
- Regional variations → specialized sub-knowledge

This creates positive feedback. Professional Colleague Agents
become more valuable as more practitioners use them. This
favors first-movers and platforms with broad reach.

For a foundation like OKWF, this means: deploy widely (and
freely if possible) to capture network effects, even at cost
of forgoing per-user revenue.

### 5.4. Competitive Dynamics

Professional Colleague Agents tend toward:

- **One dominant per profession**, due to knowledge compounding
- **Low switching costs at individual level**, but high
  institutional switching costs
- **Commoditization of Layer B** as LLM providers proliferate
- **Strong moat at Layer A** for established players

Implication: First-mover advantage is real. Quality of Layer
A determines long-term position. Expert curation network is
the competitive asset.

### 5.5. Sustainability Considerations

Professional Colleague Agents have potentially sustainable
unit economics. With reasonable usage:

- Layer A maintenance: ~500 hours/year × $100/hour = $50,000
- LLM costs: ~$30/practitioner/month × 10,000 practitioners
  × 12 months = $3,600,000
- Interface and infrastructure: ~$200,000/year
- Operations: ~$300,000/year

Total annual operating cost for a profession of 10,000
practitioners: approximately $4 million.

If practitioners pay $30/month, revenue: $3,600,000. Roughly
break-even on subscription alone.

If foundations subsidize Layer A and operations, individual
practitioners might pay only $5/month. Foundation cost:
$1.5 million/year.

These are rough estimates but order-of-magnitude reasonable.
Foundation-funded Professional Colleague Agents are
economically viable for moderately sized professions.
