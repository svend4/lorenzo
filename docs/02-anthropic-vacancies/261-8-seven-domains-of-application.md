## 8. Seven Domains of Application

Composite Skills Agents apply naturally to professions where 
practice typically combines specializations. We identify seven 
domains where the pattern fits especially well.

### 8.1. Domain 1 — Music Education and Performance

**Sub-agent specializations might include**: Classical 
composition, jazz improvisation, electronic music production, 
specific instruments (piano, violin, voice, etc.), genres 
(folk traditions, contemporary, baroque), pedagogy (children, 
adults, beginners, advanced), technical skills (music theory, 
ear training, sight-reading), accompanying disciplines (audio 
engineering, music history, ethnomusicology).

**Typical configurations**: A jazz pianist who teaches has 
different needs from a classical violinist, who has different 
needs from an electronic music producer. Each builds their 
own configuration from the available sub-agents.

**Existing analog**: This pattern is already embodied in how 
musicians actually train — studying with multiple teachers in 
different specialties — but no AI infrastructure currently 
supports it.

### 8.2. Domain 2 — Visual and Creative Arts

**Sub-agent specializations might include**: Drawing fundamentals, 
painting techniques, digital illustration, animation, sculpture, 
photography, color theory, composition, art history (by period 
or movement), specific tools (Adobe Creative Suite, Procreate, 
Blender), business skills (gallery work, commission management, 
intellectual property).

**Typical configurations**: A digital illustrator who also does 
traditional sketching and runs a small business has very 
different needs from a fine arts painter focused on gallery 
representation.

**Bridge to existing infrastructure**: Many artists already 
combine multiple educational sources (online courses, traditional 
training, peer learning). Composite agents formalize and 
augment this practice.

### 8.3. Domain 3 — Specialized Legal Practice

**Sub-agent specializations might include**: Specific area of 
law (social, criminal, family, immigration), procedural 
expertise (specific courts, jurisdictions), client population 
(elderly, disabled, immigrants, children), language (German, 
English, Russian, etc.), technical skills (drafting, oral 
advocacy, negotiation), supporting fields (medical understanding, 
psychological assessment).

**Typical configurations**: A lawyer specializing in disability 
rights for migrants speaks both German and the migrant's 
language and has medical understanding for assessing claims — 
a combination requiring multiple sub-agents.

**Practical pilot**: This is the SGB Advocate Colleague case 
discussed in detail in Section 7.5.

### 8.4. Domain 4 — Medical Practice with Multiple Specialties

**Sub-agent specializations might include**: Specific organ 
systems, types of conditions (acute, chronic, infectious, 
autoimmune), patient populations (pediatric, geriatric, 
prenatal), specialized procedures, diagnostic interpretation 
(radiology, lab work, pathology), specific subspecialties 
(rheumatology, immunology, infectious diseases).

**Typical configurations**: A primary care physician working 
in a rural clinic with elderly patients faces different 
challenges from a specialist working in tertiary care. Even 
within specialties, working with specific patient populations 
requires different configurations.

**Critical caveat**: Medical configurations require especially 
rigorous quality assurance and explicit boundaries around what 
the composite agent can support.

### 8.5. Domain 5 — Technical Architecture and Engineering

**Sub-agent specializations might include**: Programming languages, 
architecture patterns, deployment platforms, security 
disciplines, performance engineering, testing approaches, 
specific application domains (fintech, medtech, e-commerce, 
infrastructure), compliance regimes.

**Typical configurations**: An engineer specializing in 
high-performance distributed systems for medical applications 
needs specific combinations: distributed systems, performance 
engineering, plus medical compliance, plus security 
requirements specific to healthcare.

**Bridge to existing tools**: Many engineering teams already 
informally combine specialist resources (Stack Overflow, 
language-specific documentation, specialist consultants). 
Composite agents formalize this.

### 8.6. Domain 6 — Academic Research and Cross-Disciplinary Work

**Sub-agent specializations might include**: Specific 
methodologies (quantitative, qualitative, mixed methods), 
research traditions, statistical approaches, computational 
methods, writing conventions for specific journals or fields, 
literature search across databases, ethical frameworks.

**Typical configurations**: A computational biologist working 
on epidemiology with humanities co-authors faces unique 
configuration needs combining biology, computer science, 
epidemiological methods, and humanities perspectives.

**Profession evolution**: Academic research increasingly bridges 
fields. Composite agents support this trend rather than 
fighting it.

### 8.7. Domain 7 — Therapeutic Practice and Mental Health

**Sub-agent specializations might include**: Therapeutic 
modalities (CBT, somatic experiencing, narrative therapy, 
gestalt), specific conditions (trauma, addiction, eating 
disorders, mood disorders), client populations (children, 
adolescents, elderly, couples, groups), supporting frameworks 
(neuroscience, attachment theory, family systems), cultural 
competencies, specific skills (crisis intervention, group 
facilitation).

**Typical configurations**: A therapist working with adolescents 
who have eating disorders in immigrant families requires 
specific combinations spanning therapeutic modalities, 
developmental psychology, eating disorder specifics, and 
cultural competency.

**Critical caveat**: Mental health practice requires especially 
careful attention to where AI support is appropriate (research, 
documentation, knowledge lookup) versus where it is not 
(clinical judgment, therapeutic relationship). Composite agents 
operate strictly in the support role.

### 8.8. Pattern Across Domains

What unites these domains:
- Practice is deeply specialized, but specialization combines 
  rather than replaces general profession competency
- Practitioners typically span 10-30 distinct specializations
- Each specialization is shared with other practitioners but 
  configurations are individually distinctive
- Existing professional development already follows this 
  pattern; composite agents formalize and enable it more 
  broadly

The pattern is general. We expect Composite Skills Agents to 
become standard infrastructure across most skilled professions 
within 5-10 years.
